About OpenClimateMap

Summary

OpenClimateMap is an interactive web application that visualizes global climate data on an interactive map. It provides access to historical climate observations and future climate projections based on the latest scientific research and climate models. The application presents complex climate data in an accessible, user-friendly format, making it easier to understand how our climate is changing across different regions of the world.

Purpose

The goal of OpenClimateMap is to make high-quality climate data freely accessible to everyone. Whether you're a researcher, student, educator, policy maker, or simply curious about climate change, this tool helps you explore and visualize climate patterns and projections for any location on Earth. By providing an intuitive interface to scientific climate data, we aim to support informed decision-making and increase understanding of climate change.

How to Use

Exploring the Map

  • Navigate: Pan by clicking and dragging the map. Zoom using your mouse wheel or the +/- buttons.
  • Click on the map: Click anywhere to see detailed climate data for that location, including monthly values and long-term trends.

Selecting Data Layers

  • Variable: Choose which climate variable to display (temperature, precipitation, etc.)
  • Time Period: Select historical data or future projections for different time periods
  • Scenario: For future projections, choose a climate scenario (SSP) representing different emission pathways
  • Model: View individual climate models or ensemble means (averages across multiple models)

Viewing Climate Comparisons

  • Difference Maps: View the projected change between historical periods and future projections to see how climate is expected to change
  • Charts: When you click on a location, interactive charts show monthly patterns and time series data

Data Sources

OpenClimateMap uses peer-reviewed, scientific climate data from trusted international sources:

Historical Climate Data

  • WorldClim (1970-2000): High-resolution historical climatologies from WorldClim
  • CRU TS (Climatic Research Unit Time-Series): 30-year climate averages by Mitchell, T.D. (2022): Climatic Research Unit CRU TS 2.1 dataset of gridded month-by-month observed climate (Jan. 1901- Dec. 2002). Centre for Environmental Data Analysis

Climate Projections (Future Predictions)

  • CMIP6 (Coupled Model Intercomparison Project Phase 6): State-of-the-art climate model projections from the Copernicus Climate Data Store
  • WorldClim Future: Downscaled CMIP6 projections (2021-2100) with increased spatial resolution from WorldClim

Climate Scenarios (Shared Socioeconomic Pathways)

Future climate projections are based on different Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSP) that represent possible future greenhouse gas emission trajectories. Each scenario combines assumptions about population growth, economic development, technological progress, and climate policy:

  • SSP1-2.6 (Low Emissions): A sustainable development pathway with strong climate action. Global CO₂ emissions are cut to net zero around 2050, limiting warming to approximately 1.5-2°C by 2100. This scenario assumes rapid transitions to renewable energy, improved energy efficiency, and international cooperation.
  • SSP2-4.5 (Moderate Emissions): A middle-of-the-road scenario where current trends continue with some climate action. Emissions remain roughly at current levels before declining mid-century, resulting in warming of approximately 2-3°C by 2100. This represents moderate progress on sustainability goals.
  • SSP3-7.0 (Medium-High Emissions): A fragmented world with limited climate action and regional rivalry. Emissions continue to rise throughout the century, leading to warming of approximately 3-4°C by 2100. This scenario assumes slow technological development and limited international cooperation.
  • SSP5-8.5 (High Emissions): A fossil-fueled development pathway with minimal climate policy. Emissions continue to rise rapidly, resulting in warming of approximately 4-5°C or more by 2100. This scenario assumes continued reliance on fossil fuels and high energy consumption.

Note: The numbers (e.g., 2.6, 4.5) represent the approximate radiative forcing (in W/m²) expected by 2100, which is a measure of the Earth's energy imbalance caused by greenhouse gases.

Open Source

OpenClimateMap is a free and open source project released under the MIT License. The entire source code is publicly available on GitHub, and contributions are welcome.

Non-Profit & Independent

OpenClimateMap is run as a non-profit initiative. Hosting costs are sponsored by individuals who believe in making climate data accessible to everyone. The project is completely independent and not affiliated with any commercial entity, political organization, or special interest group.

Community-Driven

We welcome contributions from developers, climate scientists, designers, and anyone passionate about climate communication. Whether you want to report a bug, suggest a feature, or contribute code, please visit our GitHub repository.
